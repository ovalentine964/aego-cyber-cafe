## Why Most Kenyan CVs Don't Work

Before we get into the how-to, let's talk about what goes wrong:

1. **Too long** — A 5-page CV for someone with 2 years of experience? Recruiters won't read it.
2. **Too generic** — The same CV for every job. No customization.
3. **No achievements** — Just listing duties instead of showing results.
4. **Poor formatting** — Hard to read, messy layout, inconsistent fonts.
5. **Typos and grammar errors** — Instant disqualification.
6. **Missing keywords** — Many companies use software (ATS) to scan CVs. Wrong keywords = auto-rejected.

As we say, *maisha ni presentation* — life is about presentation. Your CV is your presentation.

## The Perfect CV Structure for 2026

Here's the structure that works for the Kenyan job market:

### 1. Contact Information (Top of Page)
- Full name (bold, larger font)
- Phone number (with country code: +254)
- Professional email address (not sweetgirl254@gmail.com)
- Location (e.g., "Nyatike, Migori County" — this shows employers where you are)
- LinkedIn profile (optional but recommended)

### 2. Professional Summary (3-4 Lines)
This is your elevator pitch. Write it last, but put it first.

**Bad example:** *"Hardworking individual seeking a challenging position to utilize my skills and experience."*

**Good example:** *"Results-driven sales professional with 3 years of experience in FMCG distribution across Migori County. Grew territory revenue by 40% in 2025. Looking to bring the same energy and results to a regional sales manager role."*

See the difference? One is vague. The other is specific, measurable, and confident.

### 3. Work Experience (Most Recent First)
For each job, include:
- Job title
- Company name
- Dates (Month/Year – Month/Year)
- 3-5 bullet points describing what you achieved (not just what you did)

**Use the STAR method for bullet points:**
- **S**ituation: What was the context?
- **T**ask: What was your responsibility?
- **A**ction: What did you do?
- **R**esult: What was the measurable outcome?

**Example:**
- ❌ *"Responsible for sales in the territory"*
- ✅ *"Increased sales in Nyatike sub-county by 35% in 6 months by establishing 12 new retail partnerships and implementing a weekly restocking schedule"*

Numbers are your friend. Use them.

### 4. Education
- Institution name
- Qualification (e.g., Bachelor of Economics & Statistics)
- Dates attended
- Relevant coursework or achievements (if you're a recent graduate)

### 5. Skills
- List 6-10 relevant skills
- Mix technical and soft skills
- Include tools you know (Excel, QuickBooks, specific software)
- Match these to the job description

### 6. Certifications & Training (If Any)
- Professional certifications
- Short courses
- Online courses (Coursera, LinkedIn Learning, etc.)

### 7. References
- "Available upon request" is fine
- Or include 2 references with name, title, phone, and email

## CV Writing Tips Specific to Kenya

### Tailor It for Each Job
I know it's extra work, but a customized CV beats a generic one every time. Read the job description. Use their keywords. Highlight relevant experience.

### Keep It to 2 Pages Maximum
For most Kenyan job applications, 2 pages is the sweet spot. One page if you're fresh from campus.

### Use a Clean, Modern Design
No clip art. No fancy borders. No photos (unless specifically requested). Clean fonts (Calibri, Arial, or Garamond), consistent formatting, enough white space.

### Include Your County
Many employers in Kenya are looking for specific locations. If the job is in Migori, and you're in Nyatike — say so. It shows you're local and available.

### Language Matters
Write in professional English. Avoid Sheng in your CV (even if the job is casual). But don't overcomplicate it either — clear, simple language wins.

### Check, Check, Check
Typos kill CVs. Read it three times. Then have someone else read it. Then read it again.

## Fresh Graduates — Your CV Isn't Empty, You Just Need to Reframe

If you just graduated and think you have "nothing to put" — think again:

- **University projects** — Group assignments, research papers, presentations
- **Internships** — Even unpaid ones count
- **Volunteer work** — Church, community, school events
- **Leadership roles** — Class representative, club president, student leader
- **Skills** — What software do you know? What languages do you speak?
- **Part-time work** — Side hustles, casual jobs, family business

Reframe everything as experience. Because it is.
